AI Readiness Audit.

We map your workflows, find where AI actually pays, and hand you a plan you own, whether or not you build it with us.

What it is

Two to four weeks inside your real operation. Not a survey. Not a maturity model. Not a slide deck about the future of work.

We start where the work actually happens. Where the time goes, where the money leaks, where a person is doing work a machine should be doing. We walk the workflows with the people who run them and put a cost on each one, so the plan rests on your numbers instead of someone’s framework.

What comes out is a phased plan you own outright. Build it with us, build it with another firm, build it in-house. The plan is yours either way, priced and sequenced so you can act on it without us in the room.

And sometimes the right call is to wait a quarter. When the numbers say so, the audit says so, in writing. You will not be talked into building something your own data does not support. What the audit contains — and deliberately doesn’t — is spelled out in the audit guide.

Who it’s for

Businesses of 5 to 100 people, $1M to $50M in revenue, past the point where the owner can hold everything in their head, and not yet big enough for a Fortune 500 budget.

An example build

Intake agent: qualifies the request before a person touches it. Reads the incoming email or form, asks for the details that are missing, and routes the job to the right person with a clean summary attached. The win: every request arrives qualified, summarized, and routed before a person reads it.

Illustrative builds, not client claims. What we’d actually scope is whatever your free assessment shows is worth automating first. If the audit finds something worth building, the Implementation Sprint builds it.

Service 02 · 4–12 weeks · $12,000–$45,000

AI Implementation Sprint.

Agents, automations, and integrations, all tested inside your real operation, with your team trained to run them.

What it is

Small, working, tested. You watch it run and earn your trust before it touches anything that matters.

A sprint takes the workflows worth automating and builds the thing that fixes them. Agents that draft the repetitive decisions and route the judgment calls back to a person. Automations between the systems you already run. Integrations that pull your data into one source of truth instead of five systems that disagree.

One team runs it from kickoff to handoff. The advisor who scopes your sprint is the one who builds it; no junior team quietly takes over after you sign. Each workflow lands in about 90 days, because a project without a deadline is a project that dies.

And before any of it starts, you get the exact price in writing. It does not move after we start. Handoff means your team trained to run all of it, and ownership of all of it.

Who it’s for

Teams of 5 to 100 people, $1M to $50M in revenue, with workflows worth automating and named. Most arrive by way of the audit. Some already know exactly what they want built and skip straight to scoping.

Service 03 · Ongoing · Monthly · no lock-in

Managed Services.

We keep what we built healthy, watch what changes, and evolve the systems as your business does. Optional, never required.

What it is

Models shift. Vendors change their APIs and their prices. A workflow that ran clean in March can drift by September. Managed Services means someone is watching for that before it costs you anything: monitoring on every deployed system, maintenance when the ground moves underneath it, and new automations as the work itself changes.

A retainer should survive on usefulness, not paperwork. If we are not earning the fee in a given month, you should be able to act on that immediately. So you can.

And because you own everything we build, leaving is clean. No license to surrender, no hostage data, no exit fee. The month it stops paying is the month you stop paying.

Who it’s for

You could put a person on this. At 5 to 100 employees, most businesses should not. The retainer buys the watching without the headcount, and your team stays trained to run everything either way.

An example

Operations data sync: a build like this touches three vendors’ software, and any of the three can change without asking you first. Managed Services is the watch on all of it: the same job keeps showing the same status everywhere, even as the tools underneath it move.

We manage what we build, so the road here runs through the audit and the sprint. For a system we built and now run, see the MARCUS case.
